# 1 Introduction

## 1.1 Background and Motivation

The rapid expansion of tertiary computing education over the past decade has placed considerable strain on academic institutions worldwide. Enrolments in computer science and information technology programmes have grown substantially, with universities such as Nanyang Technological University (NTU) routinely teaching cohorts of several hundred students per module. This growth, while indicative of the discipline's importance to modern economies, has created a structural imbalance: the number of qualified instructors and teaching assistants has not scaled in proportion to student enrolments. The consequence is a persistent and well-documented bottleneck in the assessment cycle, whereby the grading of student work becomes an increasingly time-intensive and cognitively demanding task for academic staff.

Within the domain of software engineering education, the Unified Modelling Language (UML) occupies a foundational role. UML diagrams — including use case, class, sequence, activity, and state machine diagrams — are used extensively to teach systems analysis, object-oriented design, and software architecture. Yet despite their pedagogical importance, UML diagram assignments present a particularly acute grading challenge. Unlike multiple-choice questions, which admit deterministic, automated evaluation, UML diagrams are richly structured artefacts that require an assessor to examine spatial relationships, notational correctness, semantic intent, and adherence to a marking rubric simultaneously. A single cohort of two hundred students submitting an assignment containing five UML diagram questions produces one thousand individual diagrams requiring evaluation. Even at a conservative estimate of ten minutes per diagram, this represents over one hundred and sixty hours of grading effort — a burden that, in practice, is compressed into the days immediately following an assessment deadline, when teaching staff are simultaneously managing lectures, consultations, and research commitments.

The subjectivity inherent in UML grading compounds this burden. Different markers may interpret the same rubric criterion differently, particularly for higher-order elements such as the appropriateness of a chosen abstraction or the completeness of a sequence flow. Inconsistency between markers is a recognised threat to assessment validity, and in large classes where grading is distributed across a team of teaching assistants with varying levels of experience, maintaining inter-rater reliability is a non-trivial organisational challenge. Students, too, are affected: delayed feedback diminishes the educational value of an assessment, as the cognitive window during which corrective feedback is most effectively internalised closes rapidly after the assessment event itself.

Contemporary learning management systems (LMSs) such as Canvas, Moodle, and Blackboard have addressed parts of this problem by automating the grading of objective question types. Multiple-choice questions can be evaluated instantaneously, and some platforms support rudimentary pattern-matching for short textual answers. However, none of these platforms provides native support for the structured, rubric-based evaluation of UML diagrams. Diagram submissions, where accepted at all, are typically handled as file uploads that are then routed to a human marker, replicating the manual workflow entirely. This gap is not incidental; it reflects the genuine difficulty of interpreting diagrammatic content programmatically using traditional rule-based approaches.

The recent emergence of large language models (LLMs) with multimodal capabilities has materially changed what is technically feasible in this domain. Models such as OpenAI's GPT-4o and Anthropic's Claude 3.5 Sonnet are capable of processing both textual rubric criteria and visual diagram representations simultaneously, producing structured assessments that assign partial marks, identify specific errors, and generate explanatory feedback. Early research into LLM-assisted grading has demonstrated promising alignment between model-generated scores and human expert judgements for certain assessment types, and the potential for these tools to serve as scalable grading assistants — not as replacements for human academic judgement, but as a first-pass evaluation layer that dramatically reduces the manual effort required — is increasingly recognised in the educational technology literature.

Nevertheless, significant practical barriers have impeded the adoption of LLM-assisted assessment in institutional settings. Data privacy regulations and institutional data governance policies in many jurisdictions — including Singapore's Personal Data Protection Act — impose restrictions on the transmission of student assessment data to external cloud services. For many universities, the prospect of routing student submissions through commercial API endpoints raises legitimate concerns about data sovereignty and compliance. This creates a demand for on-premise deployment architectures: systems that can be hosted entirely within an institution's own infrastructure, with no dependency on external cloud providers beyond the LLM API call itself, which can be managed through institutional agreements. Furthermore, no publicly available, end-to-end platform currently integrates traditional assessment types (MCQ, written, coding) with AI-powered UML diagram grading within a single, cohesive system that addresses the full assessment lifecycle from course creation to grade publication.

It is within this context that the present project is situated. There exists a clear and compelling need for a purpose-built, full-stack assessment platform that addresses the specific pedagogical and operational requirements of computing education at scale — one that treats UML diagram grading not as an afterthought but as a first-class feature, and that does so in a manner that preserves human academic oversight, supports institutional deployment constraints, and integrates seamlessly with the existing assessment workflows of lecturers and teaching staff.

## 1.2 Problem Statement

The central problem addressed by this project is the absence of a widely adopted, integrated assessment platform that combines conventional question types with automated, AI-assisted grading of UML diagrams. While academic research has explored automated UML assessment in various forms, the existing body of work is characterised by significant limitations. The majority of prior automated grading approaches are confined to a single diagram type — most commonly the class diagram — and rely on structural comparison algorithms that are sensitive to superficial representational differences rather than semantic equivalence. These approaches do not generalise across diagram types, cannot accommodate the natural variation in valid student responses to an open-ended design problem, and have not been integrated into production-ready assessment platforms accessible to non-specialist academic staff.

A related gap concerns the absence of standardised benchmarks for rubric-based UML grading. As has been observed in recent discussions within the computing education research community — including at the CSEDU 2025 symposium — there is no publicly available, curated dataset of student UML submissions with corresponding expert-assigned rubric scores that would enable rigorous, reproducible evaluation of automated grading approaches. This absence makes it difficult to compare competing methods objectively and slows the development of the field.

At the institutional level, schools require more than a grading algorithm. They require a complete, deployable platform that manages the full assessment lifecycle: the organisation of courses, the construction of question pools, the configuration of assignments with appropriate access controls and timing constraints, the collection and storage of student submissions, the orchestration of automated grading processes, the review and adjudication of AI-generated scores by human staff, and the publication of finalised grades to students. Building such a platform demands not only machine learning expertise but also substantial software engineering effort in full-stack web development, database design, authentication and authorisation, background job processing, and DevOps. The combination of these requirements within a single student project represents both the ambition and the practical contribution of this work.

## 1.3 Scope and Objectives

The scope of this project encompasses the design, implementation, and empirical evaluation of a full-stack web-based assessment platform tailored to the needs of computing education. The platform supports four distinct assignment types: multiple-choice questions (MCQ), written (essay-style) questions, coding questions, and UML diagram questions. For the latter two types — written and UML — the platform integrates LLM-assisted grading as a core feature, employing structured output validation via Zod schemas to ensure that model responses conform to the expected rubric format before being surfaced to human reviewers.

A defining design principle of the platform is the preservation of human-in-the-loop oversight. AI-generated grades are presented to staff as suggestions rather than final scores; each suggestion must be explicitly accepted, modified, or rejected by a human marker before it is committed to the grade record. This approach is intended to maintain academic integrity and to ensure that the system augments rather than supplants the professional judgement of teaching staff.

The platform addresses the full assessment lifecycle. Administrators may create institutions and manage system-wide settings, including the selection and configuration of LLM providers. Staff members — differentiated into lecturer, teaching assistant, and laboratory executive roles — may create courses, build question pools, configure assignments with specific access windows, time limits, and focus-monitoring controls, and review student submissions and AI-generated grades. Students may access their enrolled courses, attempt assignments within the configured constraints, and view feedback and grades once published. Role-based access control (RBAC) is enforced at both the API and the user interface levels to ensure that each actor can access only the functionality appropriate to their role.

The system is designed for on-premise deployment, packaged as a multi-container Docker application that can be orchestrated using Docker Compose for single-node deployments or Kubernetes for larger institutional environments. The LLM integration is configurable per institution, supporting OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google as grading backends, with token usage and associated costs tracked and reported to administrators.

The formal objectives of the project are as follows. First, to design and develop a production-quality, full-stack web-based assessment platform implementing the architecture and features described above. Second, to implement LLM-assisted grading pipelines for written and UML diagram question types, incorporating structured output validation to ensure reliable, parseable model responses. Third, to embed human-in-the-loop review mechanisms throughout the grading workflow, ensuring that automated suggestions remain subject to academic oversight. Fourth, to conduct a formal empirical experiment comparing multiple LLM models — spanning flagship and mid-tier offerings from OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google — across dimensions of grading accuracy, consistency, and cost-effectiveness for UML diagram assessment, thereby contributing a modest empirical dataset to a field that currently lacks standardised benchmarks. Fifth, to deploy the completed platform in a containerised, on-premise-ready configuration and to evaluate its functional correctness and non-functional characteristics through systematic testing.

These objectives collectively reflect the dual nature of the project: it is simultaneously a software engineering exercise in full-stack web development and a modest empirical investigation into the practical utility of LLMs as grading assistants in computing education.

## 1.4 Report Organisation

The remainder of this report is organised as follows. Chapter 2 presents a review of the relevant literature, examining prior work on automated assessment systems, UML diagram grading, LLM-assisted evaluation, and the pedagogical considerations that inform the platform's design. Chapter 3 describes the system design, covering the overall architecture, database schema, API design, authentication and authorisation model, and the design of the LLM grading pipeline. The design decisions reported in Chapters 3 and 4 are grounded in the gaps identified in the literature reviewed in Chapter 2; readers who wish to understand the rationale behind specific architectural choices are encouraged to read Chapter 2 before proceeding to the design chapters.
